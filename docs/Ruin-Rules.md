## Making Skill Checks:


There are 3 types of skill checks that you can make: Static Checks, Dynamic Checks, and Contested Checks

### Static Checks

For simple tasks that are done over a duration of time or without much pressure, you would make a static check. These checks are usually not to determine whether the action succeeds or not, but instead how long it takes, how much resources it costs, and the degree of success. Add the associated stat's score + SB to calculate the result. If the DM determines there may be a degree of luck in the check, they can ask you to add a certain die to the roll, in which case they will increase the DC accordingly.

### Dynamic Checks

For a swifter, more stressful situation, a dynamic check will be made. These checks are usually made to determine if an action succeeds or not, as well as how well it succeeds. Roll the associated stat's die + SB to calculate the result.

### Contested Checks

For tasks which are contested by another creature, such as attempting to grapple someone or beat them to grabbing an item, both creatures will make contested checks. If not indicated, both creatures make the same skill check, rolling it as a Dynamic Check. Some cases will require the contester to make a Static Check instead or to use a different skill entirely, such as attempting to spot a hidden creature. Whichever creature rolls higher succeeds while the other fails. On a tie, the creature attempting to avoid the effect succeeds.

### Degrees of Success

Whenever the result of a check is equal to or greater than the DC, it is a success.

If the result is greater than the DC by 5 or more, it is a critical success

If the result is lesser than the DC, it is a failure

If the result is lesser than the DC by 5 or more, it is a critical failure.

Certain skill checks will have specific effects on critical successes and failures, but most will be determined by the GM.

### Attacks

Unlike other systems, all hostile actions are considered attacks in Ruin. Whenever you make an attack with a weapon, cast a spell, or try to charm someone, its considered an attack. There are the following classifications for attacks:

Weapon Attacks: Attacks made with a weapon
Spell Attacks: Attacks made from a spell
Melee Attacks: Attacks made within Reach
Ranged Attacks: Attacks not made within Reach
Contested Attacks: Attacks which require a contested roll from the target
Binary Attacks: Attacks which do not deal damage, but the damage roll is simply used to determine whether the attack hits or misses.

Some attacks can be multiple of these classifications at a time, like Flaming Blade, which is a Melee Spell Weapon attack, or Enthrall which is a Binary Spell Attack.

Naming Convention Order

Binary > Contested > Melee/Ranged > Spell > Weapon

Whenever you roll an attack, you are not rolling to see whether it hits (unless its a Binary Attack). Instead, you are simply rolling damage. Creatures will use reactions to attempt to negate this damage or effect, and in the case of Binary Attacks need to reduce the 'damage' to 0 to negate its effect

### Checks


Checks are made to determine the level of success in achieving a certain action. Skill checks are a type of check which uses your skill bonus, and ability checks are a type of check which uses your ability die.

Whenever you make a check, it will be based on one of the four stats, Might, Dexterity, Will, and Presence. You have a die for each of these skills as determined by your class which also effects your ability to make Saves.

### Saves


Saves are a special classification of rolls which are provoked by harmful effects. Every creature can take a reaction to make one of the following saves

Defend: Roll your Might Die
Avoid: Roll your Dexterity Die
Resist: Roll your Will Die
Persist: Roll your Presence Die

Certain features like the Thief's Untouchable feature and wearing a shield allow you to gain bonuses specific saves, while others like the Powerful effect grant a bonus to all saves.

## Tags

Features, abilities, and attacks, along with other similar things, all have things called Tags. Tags are a list of groupings for these features which are used to easily refer to a selection of features. For example, attacks and features with the Physical tag are used to represent that they are physical effects so that certain features like the Block reaction can be used on them. Similarly, some attacks and features have the non-physical tag, which represents that they are not physical effects that can be interacted with in certain manners like the Block reaction.

Tags can also be used to indicate what class, race, or spell list they belong to, and generally serve as an indexing tool.

Conditions are harmful or positive effects which afflict a creature. Whenever a condition is inflicted, it will give an end condition, such as "The creature is blinded until the end of its next turn". A creature can only be afflicted by a instance of a condition at a time.

Some conditions will instead give something like "The creature is Burning (3)". This is called a stackable condition, meaning it can be repeatedly afflicted by a this condition, each time either extending the duration, severity, or both. The format of these conditions is "Condition (X)" where Condition is the name of the condition and X is the Severity. Each condition will use X in place of the Severity, and some will provide conditions in which X decreased, indicated by "X - 1" or "X - (the result)". At the end of the creature's turn, X - 1 unless otherwise specified. If X = 0, the creature is no longer afflicted by the condition.

Whenever you gain the same condition which has a severity from two different sources, the Severity from each one is added. For example if you already have Burnout (2) and you make an attack, you gain Burnout (1). This means you now have Burnout (3).

## Combat

So words have failed and we are resorting to violence. Here's how to run a combat.

## Parties

Before combat even can think of beginning, you need to determine Parties. Parties are a grouping of creatures which are allied and can reasonably communicate with each other before and during combat. They can also be used to group a large amount of creatures to avoid managing them all at once. For example, if you are running 15 goblins and a goblin leader, maybe you would like to have 3 Parties of 5 goblins, and the Goblin leader alone. Alternatively you could place the Goblin leader in one of the existing Parties. 

Parties serve mainly as a management tool to break up combat and group creatures based on alignment and awareness. Most of the time, you will have the PCs in a single party, but sometimes due to the party being split, or a member being unaware, you may say they are split.

For the sake of the combat rules, PCs will refer to a party composing of at least one Player Character, as well as any parties which are allied with them.

### Ambush:

Once you've determined the Parties, you can then determine which of them are Ambushed. Ambushes are a special type of encounter which begins by a creature initiating the combat through a hostile action which every member of at least one Party was unaware of or unexpected. Use GM approval and common sense for determining if a Party is Ambushed. If a villain is monologuing and a PC suddenly throws a dagger, the villain was likely at least anticipating a combat, and so is not Ambushed. If a party is Ambushed, they start combat with Incapacitated (1).

Initiative:
Great, now with all that stuff out of the way, its time to roll initiative. Each PC chooses a party and that party elects a Leader (determined by the GM). Each party must be chosen before a PC can select a party that has already been chosen to elect an additional Leader. The PC and the Leader make contested Initiative Checks. On a success, the PC enters the Ready group and the Leader (along with its party) enters the Surprised group. On a failure, the opposite happens. All remaining parties enter the Ready group.


### Rounds:

Rounds progress as follows:

- R1: PCs in the Ready group take their turns in any order, but they must declare their turn beginning and only one turn can happen at a time. 
- R2: Hostile Parties in the Ready Group take their turns in any order, again taking only one turn at a time.
- S1: PCs in the Surprised group take their turns, again in any order but only one turn at a time.
- S2: Hostile Parties in the Surprised group take their turns, you guessed it, in any order, one turn at a time.

After each phase is complete, R1 begins again.

### Combat End:

Combat ends when the GM determines it is over. This usually happens whenever every hostile creature or PC is dead, but can also happen if either retreat or surrender. Another good practice is to 'call combat' which means to determine the combat is over when victory for the PCs is practically assured. You can ask the PCs to describe how they finish off the remaining enemies and end the combat there.


### Reach


Reach is a distance determined by an attacking creatures arm reach. By default, a creature's Reach is 5ft, but some creatures, especially larger ones, have longer Reach.


### Your Turn



On your turn, you have 3 AP (action points) and 3 reactions. Whenever you start your turn, you regain all expended AP and reactions.

You can spend AP to take actions such as move, attack, or cast a spell. Basic Actions are actions which any creature can take, even if it is not listed on their sheet or statblock. 

You can take reactions only in response to certain triggers, as explained in the specific reaction you wish to take. For example, the Block reaction requires you to be targeted by an attack with the Physical tag while wielding a shield. Reactions trigger immediately, and resolve before the triggering effect. 


## Basic Actions (1 AP)

Attack, Cast a Spell, Dodge, Grapple, Help, Hide, Improvise, Interact, Move, Search, Shove

## Reactions

Instead of rolling to hit, you roll to avoid / reduce damage. Every creature can take the following basic reactions, which one they are able to take is determined by the type of attack

Defend: You attempt to block or absorb an attack, roll your Might Die and subtract it from the damage.
Avoid: You attempt to dive or dodge an attack, roll your Dexterity Die and subtract it from the damage.
Resist: You attempt to hold back or power through a mental effect from an attack, roll your Will Die and subtract it from the damage.
Persist: You attempt to remain or subvert an effect which attempts to subdue you, roll your Presence Die and subtract it from the damage.

## Resting

Resting is a key part of adventuring. Without it, your characters would surely die of exhaustion or their injuries.

Most abilities, features, and uses of items recharge on a rest and indicate what type of rest they recharge on. If a feature recharges on a Rest, it recharges on any rest, while if it specifies which type, you must take that specific rest to regain the use of the feature.


### Short Rest:

A short rest is a brief moment of recovery which can be taken over the course of about 10 minutes which can be taken at any time, but is automatically taken after a combat provided that there are 10 minutes without another combat beginning. A short rest is completed as long as you are not Incapacitated or Downed.

### Brief Rest:

A Brief Rest is a longer period of time often taken at the end of an adventuring day. It consists of at least 6 hours of sleeping and 2 hours of light activity such as cooking, reading, crafting or talking. You must not be Incapacitated or Downed and must be in a safe designated place with decent comfort such as a camp or inn. You regain HP, Mental, and Grave equal to your Might, Will, and Presence Scores respectively, and Injured (-1).

You can only take a Brief Rest once per day and if a day ends without you taking a brief rest, you gain Exhaustion (1).

### Long Rest

A Long Rest is an extended period of time, often taken during a period of downtime or after a long journey. It consists of at least 8 hours of sleeping and 16 hours of light activity such as cooking, reading, crafting or talking. You must sleep in a safe designated space with great comfort such as an inn and spend the rest of your time in a safe place such as a city or town. You regain all of your HP, Mental, and Grave and Injured (-2).

You can only take a Long Rest once every three days.

### Interrupting Rests

If a Rest is interrupted by combat, you gain Exhaustion (1) at the end of the combat. As long as the Rest is resumed within 10 minutes of the combat beginning, you can retain the benefits of the rest. Otherwise, you must start the rest over again.
